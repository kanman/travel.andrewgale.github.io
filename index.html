Heron Island

Heron Island is a natural coral cay, right on the Southern Great Barrier Reef, 2 hours by boat from Gladstone in Queensland.

The island is a national park, and natural coral cay, featuring an extraordinary range of birdlife and sealife, found nowhere else in the world.

Host to a population of 4000 Green and Loggerhead turtles, that nest on the beaches from October through to January, the lifecycle of these amazing creatures can be observed and studied.

Little baby hatchlings can be seen scurrying into the water from January each year.
The abundant range of sealife on the island includes sea turtles, eagle rays, manta rays, reef sharks (harmless) and maori wrasse fish.

About Sea Turtles

From October to March each year, Heron Island becomes the breeding home to hundreds of Green and Loggerhead turtles. These giants of the sea return to the island some 30 years after they hatched here, continuing the circle of life. Green turtles make up around 98% of the nesting population.

There are several ways to differentiate between them, the easiest by looking at their tracks: Green sea turtles move both front flippers at the same time, creating paired parallel markings in the sand. Loggerheads move one flipper at a time, creating alternating flipper marks with a wavy line running through the centre.

The turtle hatchlings usually begin to emerge from their nests and make a dash for the ocean from December through to late March. The majority of predation of turtle hatchlings occurs in the water, therefore it is vital they have the chance to increase their body temperature as they make their way down the beach to be prepared to swim fast once they enter the cold water.
